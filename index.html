Course Description

This course systematically explores the channels of monetary transmission, the operational balance sheet dynamics of modern central banking, and macroeconomic trade-offs captured in the Impossible Trilemma. It is structured around the Mishkin framework and the IMF's semi-structural QPM forecasting approach. Students will learn how central banks utilize open market operations and interest rates to manage inflation and stabilize output.

Lecture 1: Functions of Money & Liquidity Hierarchy

Money resolves the fundamental "double coincidence of wants" problem inherent in barter economies, drastically lowering transaction and search costs. In macroeconomic theory, money is defined through three classical pillars: Medium of Exchange (facilitating trade), Unit of Account (standardizing relative values), and Store of Value (transferring purchasing power across time).

Central Banks categorize money into **Monetary Aggregates** based on their degree of liquidity. Liquidity refers to the speed, ease, and certainty with which an asset can be converted back into a primary medium of exchange.

$$M0 = \text{Currency in Circulation (Banknotes and Coins)}$$ $$M1 = M0 + \text{Demand Deposits (Highly liquid checking accounts)}$$ $$M2 = M1 + \text{Time \& Savings Deposits (Interest-bearing term deposits)}$$

Analytical Commentary: The Liquidity Continuum

Notice how shifts in aggregates alter the underlying liquidity mix of the financial system. When transactions drive physical cash or checking balances up, M1 expands immediately, reflecting increased transactional velocity. Conversely, high macroeconomic risk or enticing real yields prompt a flight toward fixed-term liabilities (Time Deposits), causing M2 to capture broader liquidity structures while transactional assets stabilize. Central banks continuously monitor this shifting frontier to anticipate inflation spikes and structural velocity shifts.

Lecture 2: Interest Rates & Money Demand

Keynes' Liquidity Preference Theory asserts that individuals hold cash for three distinct reasons: 1) Transactions Motive (day-to-day transactions), 2) Precautionary Motive (unexpected future obligations), and 3) Speculative Motive (holding cash to exploit fluctuations in bond prices and yields).

Because non-interest-bearing cash compete directly with interest-yielding bonds, money demand ($M^d$) has an inverse relationship with nominal interest rates ($i$). When interest rates rise, holding liquid cash yields a higher opportunity cost, leading actors to lock capital into bonds.

In the standard short-run model, the Money Supply ($M^s$) is strictly controlled by the Central Bank and is represented as a **vertical line**.

$$M^d = L(i, Y) \quad \text{where} \quad \frac{\partial L}{\partial i} < 0, \ \frac{\partial L}{\partial Y} > 0$$ $$P_{bond} = \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{C}{(1+i)^t} + \frac{F}{(1+i)^n}$$

Lecture 4: Balance Sheets & Open Market Operations (OMO)

Open Market Operations (OMO) serve as the primary vehicle to regulate systemic short-term interbank liquidity. A central bank purchase of government securities directly inflates the monetary base.

Through modern accounting principles, we track how central bank decisions generate exact offsetting adjustments on commercial banks' balances. To emphasize this double-entry harmony, study the synchronized balance sheets below.

Monetary Policy Tools

The most important role of the central bank is conducting monetary policy.
Monetary policy influences the aggregate economy, and the central bank regulates economic activity through:

Open market operations are the most important monetary policy tool, because they are the primary determinants of changes in interest rates and the monetary base, the main source of fluctuations in the money supply. Open market purchases expand reserves and the monetary base, thereby raising the money supply and lowering short-term interest rates. Open market sales shrink reserves and the monetary base, lowering the money supply and raising short-term interest rates. There are two types of open market operations: Dynamic open market operations are intended to change the level of reserves and the monetary base, and Defensive open market operations.

Lecture 5: Inflation Dynamics & The Phillips Curve

The Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC) demonstrates the classical trade-off between inflation ($\pi$) and unemployment ($u$). An expansion in aggregate demand pushes the economy beyond full capacity, accelerating wage inflation as labor markets tighten.

In the long run (LRPC), the trade-off dissolves. The curve becomes strictly vertical at the **Natural Rate of Unemployment ($u_n$, NAIRU)**. Attempts to artificially inflate the economy past this limit yield zero real employment gains, transferring solely into structural expected inflation.

$$\pi = \pi^e - \alpha(u - u_n) + v \quad \text{where} \quad v \text{ represents exogenous supply shocks}$$

Lecture 6: The Mundell-Fleming Impossible Trilemma

The Impossible Trinity (Mundell-Fleming Trilemma) is a foundational open-macroeconomy restriction. It asserts that a sovereign country can never simultaneously secure all three of the following policy outcomes:

  1. Free Capital Mobility (unrestricted cross-border capital allocations)
  2. Exchange Rate Stability (hard-пегged or highly-managed exchange rates)
  3. Monetary Policy Independence (autonomous setting of policy interest rates)

Mathematically, under perfect capital mobility, the Uncovered Interest Parity (UIP) holds. If a central bank attempts to peg the exchange rate, it surrenders interest-rate control directly to the base nation.

$$i_d = i_f + \frac{E^e_{t+1} - E_t}{E_t} + \rho \quad \text{where} \quad \rho \text{ represents the country risk premium}$$

Lecture 7: The Quarterly Projection Model (QPM)

IMF Framework (2026)

1. What is the QPM?

The Quarterly Projection Model (QPM) is the core macroeconomic forecasting framework used by many inflation-targeting central banks and by the IMF within the Forecasting and Policy Analysis System (FPAS)[cite: 1]. The QPM is designed to answer a simple but important question: What should the central bank do today to achieve its inflation target in the future?[cite: 1]

The QPM is a semi-structural, New Keynesian, forward-looking gap model[cite: 1]. Unlike purely statistical forecasting models, the QPM incorporates economic theory and behavioral relationships among inflation, output, interest rates, and exchange rates[cite: 1].

2. Why Do Central Banks Need a QPM?

Monetary policy affects the economy with long and variable lags[cite: 1]. A change in the policy rate today affects financial markets immediately, investment after several months, aggregate demand after multiple quarters, and inflation only after a significant delay (typically 4–8 quarters later)[cite: 1]. Thus central banks cannot focus only on current inflation; they must forecast future economic conditions[cite: 1].

3. QPM as a Gap Model

Instead of modeling macroeconomic levels directly, the framework focuses on structural deviations from equilibrium values[cite: 1]. The system tracks three core gaps:

  • Output Gap ($\hat y_t$): Measures the deviation of actual output ($Y_t$) from potential output ($Y_t^*$)[cite: 1].
  • Real Interest Rate Gap ($\hat r_t$): Deviation of actual real interest rate ($r_t$) from the neutral real interest rate ($r_t^*$)[cite: 1].
  • Exchange Rate Gap ($\hat z_t$): Measures the deviation of the real exchange rate from its long-term structural trend[cite: 1].

4. Core Structure and Equations

The IMF canonical QPM consists of four fundamental behavioral equations forming the backbone of the model[cite: 1]:

Aggregate Demand Equation (IS Curve)
$$\hat y_t = \beta_1 \hat y_{t-1} - \beta_2 MCI_t + \beta_3 \hat y_t^{f} + \varepsilon_t^y$$

The output gap depends on past output gaps, the Monetary Conditions Index ($MCI_t$), foreign output gaps ($\hat y_t^{f}$), and demand shocks ($\varepsilon_t^y$)[cite: 1]. Higher interest rates contract spending, lowering the aggregate gap[cite: 1].

Phillips Curve
$$\pi_t = \alpha_1 \pi_{t-1} + (1-\alpha_1)E_t(\pi_{t+1}) + \alpha_2 RMC_t + \varepsilon_t^{\pi}$$

Models inflation as a function of past inflation, expected inflation ($E_t(\pi_{t+1})$), real marginal costs ($RMC_t$, tied to output gaps), and supply shocks[cite: 1]. Inflation rises when demand exceeds capacity or expectations shift[cite: 1].

Uncovered Interest Parity (UIP)
$$i_t = i_t^* + E_t(\Delta e_{t+1}) + prem_t$$

Links domestic interest rates ($i_t$) and foreign interest rates ($i_t^*$), expected exchange rate depreciation ($E_t(\Delta e_{t+1})$), and risk premium ($prem_t$)[cite: 1]. If domestic rates expand, domestic assets become more attractive, stimulating capital inflows and appreciation[cite: 1].

Monetary Policy Reaction Function (Taylor Rule)
$$i_t = \gamma_1 i_{t-1} + (1-\gamma_1)[i_t^n + \gamma_2(\pi_t - \pi^*) + \gamma_3 \hat y_t] + \varepsilon_t^i$$

A Taylor-type rule determining the policy interest rate[cite: 1]. If inflation exceeds target ($\pi^*$) or output gap is positive, the central bank tightens the policy rate ($i_t$) relative to its neutral level ($i_t^n$)[cite: 1].

5. Policy Applications & Scenario Analysis

One major advantage of the QPM is scenario analysis[cite: 1]. The model allows central banks to simulate monetary policy shocks, external resource price adjustments, demand shifts, or risk premium changes[cite: 1]. This structural system assists policymakers to forecast inflation paths, evaluate interest-rate path options, and improve public communication transparency under strict inflation-targeting rules[cite: 1].
