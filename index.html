Monetary Economics (5 ECTS) • Almaty Management University

This course systematically explores the channels of monetary transmission, the operational balance sheet dynamics of modern central banking, and macroeconomic trade-offs captured in the Impossible Trilemma. Structured around the Mishkin framework and the IMF's semi-structural QPM forecasting approach.

Course Learning Outcomes (LOs):

  • LO1: Evaluate how monetary policy instruments impact commercial bank liquidity and target money supply.
  • LO2: Apply the Fisher Equation and Bond Pricing theory to interest rate fluctuations.
  • LO3: Analyze open-market structural adjustments utilizing T-Accounts.
  • LO4: Synthesize policy decisions using the Taylor Rule response function.

Required Academic Reading List

Paper 1 • Seminal Classic

M. Friedman: "The Role of Monetary Policy" (1968)

Introducing the concepts of natural rate of unemployment and exploring structural limits of expansionary policy.

Academic Overview:

Friedman presents his fundamental critique of the Phillips Curve, arguing that monetary authorities cannot permanently trade higher inflation for lower unemployment. He establishes the 'Natural Rate of Unemployment' (NAIRU) and outlines the structural constraints of discretionary expansion.

Paper 2 • Policy Rules

J. Taylor: "Discretion versus Policy Rules in Practice" (1993)

The original seminal article introducing the Taylor Rule reaction function to close output and inflation gaps.

Academic Overview:

Taylor outlines a systematic rules-based reaction function that central banks can use to adjust interest rates. The rule balances inflation deviations from target and output deviations from potential, illustrating that a predictable policy anchor preserves market confidence better than erratic discretion.

Paper 3 • International Macro

R. Mundell: "Capital Mobility and Stabilization Policy" (1963)

Foundational work framing the impossible trilemma trade-offs in open macroeconomies.

Academic Overview:

This paper forms the backbone of the Mundell-Fleming model, demonstrating that under free capital mobility, monetary policy is highly effective under flexible exchange rates but completely ineffective under a fixed currency peg.

Paper 4 • Central Banking Models

IMF: "FPAS and QPM Semi-Structural Modeling"

Practical documentation on Forecasting and Policy Analysis Systems used by central banks globally.

Academic Overview:

This IMF staff paper explains the conceptual building blocks of the Quarterly Projection Model (QPM). It maps the 4 main equations (IS Curve, Phillips Curve, Uncovered Interest Parity, and Taylor Rule) that modern central banks combine to generate forecasting scenarios.

Paper 5 • Financial Friction

B. Bernanke: "Nonmonetary Effects of the Financial Crisis"

Detailing how banking panic, liquidity squeezes, and asymmetric information disrupted credit transmission.

Academic Overview:

Bernanke highlights the critical nature of financial intermediaries. He argues that during crises, the breakdown of credit channels and bank insolvencies create severe non-monetary propagation effects that deepen macroeconomic contractions.

Paper 6 • Digital Frontier

BIS: "Central Bank Digital Currencies: Ongoing structural shifts"

A strategic review of retail and wholesale CBDC implications for the structural monetary system.

Academic Overview:

A comprehensive review of CBDC architectures by the Bank for International Settlements. The paper evaluates how direct digital liabilities change interbank clearance, money multiplier calculations, and commercial deposit stability.
